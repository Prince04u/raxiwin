Understanding Problem Gambling

Problem gambling is a recognized behavioural health condition that affects people from every background. It is characterized by an inability to control the urge to gamble despite clear negative consequences — financial, emotional, social, or professional. Like other behavioural health conditions, it exists on a spectrum, and early intervention significantly improves outcomes.

Common warning signs include: spending more time or money on gambling than intended; chasing losses; lying about gambling activity to friends or family; borrowing money to gamble or to pay off gambling debts; feeling restless or irritable when not gambling; gambling to escape from problems or negative emotions; and neglecting work, school, or family responsibilities because of gambling.

If any of these warning signs sound familiar — whether about yourself or someone close to you — please consider using the tools below or reaching out to one of the support organizations we recommend.

Tools We Provide

Every Raxiwin player has access to a comprehensive set of self-management tools. These tools are free to use, take effect immediately, and can be set up in less than a minute through your account settings.

Deposit Limits

Set a maximum amount you can deposit daily, weekly, or monthly. Limits can be reduced at any time with immediate effect. Requests to increase a limit are subject to a 24-hour cooling-off period.

Loss Limits

Cap the net amount you can lose over a given period. Once the limit is reached, you will be prevented from placing further bets until the period resets.

Wager Limits

Set a maximum total amount you can wager over a defined period, regardless of wins or losses. This is particularly useful for players who want to control time-on-site as well as spend.

Session Reminders

Configure a pop-up reminder after a chosen amount of time spent playing. The reminder displays your session length, net result, and a quick link to take a break.

Reality Checks

Receive a clear summary of your wins, losses, and play time on a regular schedule of your choosing — daily, weekly, or monthly.

Time-Outs

Take a short break of 24 hours, 7 days, 30 days, or up to 6 weeks. During a time-out your account is locked, no marketing communications are sent, and no deposits or bets can be placed.

Self-Exclusion

For longer-term breaks, self-exclusion is available for 6 months, 1 year, 5 years, or permanently. Self-exclusion takes effect immediately and cannot be reversed during the chosen period.

How We Identify At-Risk Behaviour

We do not rely solely on players to flag problems themselves. Our responsible-gaming team uses a combination of analytics and human review to identify patterns of play that may indicate harm — for example, sudden increases in deposit frequency, late-night play sessions of unusual length, repeated reversals of withdrawal requests, or frequent updates to maximum deposit limits.

When such patterns are detected, our team may reach out with a personal message offering information about our tools, suggesting a voluntary cooling-off period, or, in serious cases, applying account restrictions. These interventions are designed to be supportive, not punitive, and are always carried out with respect for the player's privacy.

Talking to Someone You Care About

Approaching a friend or family member about their gambling can be one of the hardest conversations you will ever have. There is no perfect script, but there are some principles that tend to make these conversations more constructive.

Choose a calm moment, away from the heat of a recent loss or argument. Lead with concern rather than accusation: 'I've been worried about you' opens a door that 'you have a problem' slams shut. Be specific about what you have noticed — the missed dinner, the borrowed money, the late-night sessions — rather than offering a general diagnosis.

Listen far more than you speak. Many people struggling with gambling already know something is wrong; what they need is to feel heard, not lectured. Offer to help them find professional support rather than trying to solve the problem yourself. And take care of your own wellbeing too — supporting someone with a gambling problem is exhausting, and you deserve support of your own.

Financial Safety

Practical financial measures can make a meaningful difference for players who are concerned about their gambling. Consider setting up a separate bank account used only for entertainment spending, with a fixed monthly transfer from your main account. Use a debit card rather than a credit card. Block gambling-related transactions through your bank if your bank supports this feature. Many UK and European banks now offer this option, and it is rapidly becoming available in other markets.

If your finances have already been affected, free, confidential debt advice is available through organizations such as StepChange and the National Debtline. These organizations exist precisely to help people in your situation and will not judge you.

Technology That Helps

Beyond our in-platform tools, a number of third-party technologies can help you create distance between yourself and gambling sites. Gamban, Betfilter, and GamBlock are subscription services that block access to thousands of gambling sites across your devices. Browser extensions such as BlockSite can be configured to restrict access to specific URLs during chosen hours. Operating-system level screen-time controls on iOS and Android can be configured to limit time spent on gambling apps.

We are happy to provide guidance on any of these tools through our support channels. Using them is not a sign of weakness — it is a sign of self-awareness and good planning.

Returning After a Break

Many players who take a break from gambling — whether through a time-out, self-exclusion, or simply stepping away — choose to return at some point. Returning safely is its own skill.

Before reactivating your account, take a moment to reflect on what led to the break and whether the circumstances have meaningfully changed. Consider setting a low deposit limit before you reactivate, and resist the temptation to remove it during the first few weeks. Plan in advance what you will do if you find yourself slipping back into earlier patterns — perhaps committing to a new time-out at the first warning sign.

Returning to gambling is not the right choice for everyone, and there is no shame in deciding that a permanent break is the healthier path. Our specialist team is available to talk through your situation confidentially whenever you wish.

The Science of Self-Control

Decades of research in behavioral psychology have given us a much better understanding of how self-control actually works. The single most important finding is that willpower in the moment is a limited resource — and that the most reliable way to make good decisions is to remove the need to make them in real time. Setting deposit limits in advance, configuring session reminders, and pre-committing to a budget all work because they take advantage of your calm, rational self to constrain your stressed, in-the-moment self.

Another robust finding is that reflection helps. Players who pause briefly to consider their session — how long they have been playing, whether they are ahead or behind, how they feel — make demonstrably better decisions than players who do not. This is why our session reminders include a brief summary of your activity rather than just a generic 'you've been playing for an hour' message. The numbers themselves prompt healthy reflection.

Finally, social support matters. Players who tell at least one person they trust about their gambling — even just casually, in passing — are more likely to keep their play in healthy bounds than players who keep their activity entirely private. There is no obligation to share, but if you do, you may find it easier to stay in control than you expected.

Recognizing Subtle Warning Signs

The obvious warning signs of problem gambling — borrowing money, lying about losses, missing important commitments — are well known. But there are many subtler signs that often appear earlier and that are worth being aware of.

Increased tolerance: needing to bet more than you used to in order to feel the same level of excitement. Mood swings tied to gambling outcomes that linger well beyond the session itself. A growing preoccupation with gambling between sessions — checking results, planning the next session, replaying losses in your head. A creeping social withdrawal as gambling crowds out other activities. A nagging sense that you should slow down, paired with a reluctance to actually do so. None of these signs is conclusive on its own, but a cluster of several is worth taking seriously.

Common Misconceptions

Several persistent misconceptions about gambling make it harder for players to make sound decisions. The first is the gambler's fallacy: the belief that past results influence future outcomes in games of pure chance. They do not. A roulette wheel does not 'owe' you a red after eight blacks in a row, any more than a coin owes you tails after eight heads. Each spin is independent.

The second is the illusion of control: the feeling that you can influence the outcome of a fundamentally random event through ritual, choice of timing, or skillful execution. With a few well-defined exceptions — such as basic strategy in blackjack or skill-based elements in some poker variants — you cannot. Accepting this is liberating, not depressing.

The third is hot-hand thinking: the belief that a recent run of wins indicates a personal streak that should be ridden until it ends. Streaks happen by chance in any random process. Treating them as something more is a recipe for giving back your winnings.

Understanding these biases will not make you immune to them — they are built into how the human brain processes uncertainty — but recognizing them in real time makes it easier to step back.

Financial Recovery

If gambling has affected your finances, the best advice we can offer is to stop the bleeding first and then address the existing situation methodically. Stopping the bleeding means applying meaningful self-exclusion across every site you have used, blocking gambling transactions through your bank, and removing or restricting access to credit. The objective is to make further losses impossible, not merely inconvenient.

Addressing the existing situation means seeking professional debt advice from a recognized, free service. Avoid commercial 'debt consolidation' offers that may make the situation worse. A qualified debt advisor can help you understand your rights, negotiate with creditors, and build a realistic plan for recovery. Recovery is possible, but it takes time, and the first step is almost always the hardest one.

Practical Self-Help Toolkit

We close this page with a practical toolkit you can use today to reduce the chance that your gambling causes harm. None of the items below requires permission from anyone; you can do all of them yourself in a few minutes.

Set up a separate bank account or e-wallet used only for gambling, with a fixed monthly transfer from your main account. When the monthly balance is gone, you are done for the month. This single step turns gambling from a variable cost into a fixed entertainment budget, which is exactly how it should be experienced.

Configure deposit limits, loss limits, and session reminders on every gambling site you use, including ours. Set them low. You can always lower them further; the cooling-off period exists specifically to make it harder to raise them when you are stressed.

Install one of the recognized gambling-blocker tools — Gamban, Betfilter, or GamBlock — on every device you own, and pair it with operating-system screen-time controls that limit the time you can spend on gambling-related apps. Even if you do not need these tools today, having them installed creates useful friction when you do need them.

Tell at least one person you trust about your gambling. You do not need to share details. A simple 'I'm playing a bit on Raxiwin this month' to a partner or a close friend creates accountability and reduces the secrecy that often accompanies harmful play.

Keep an honest log of your gambling for at least one month — every session, every deposit, every win, every loss, and a one-line note on how you felt afterward. People are routinely shocked by what they find when they look at the actual data, and the act of writing it down tends to change behavior even before the analysis begins.

Schedule a regular non-gambling activity that you enjoy and that takes place at the time you would otherwise be tempted to play. Substitution is far more effective than abstinence; the brain handles 'I'm doing something else' much better than it handles 'I'm not doing the thing I want to do.'

Remove gambling apps from the home screen of your phone and remove gambling bookmarks from your browser. Every extra click between the impulse and the action gives you another chance to make a calm decision.

If you experience a bad session, take a mandatory cooling-off period of at least 48 hours. Use the time-out tool to enforce it externally rather than relying on willpower. Many of the most damaging gambling episodes occur in the immediate aftermath of an upsetting loss, when chasing-loss psychology is at its peak.

Periodically review your overall situation — once a month is a reasonable cadence. Ask yourself honestly: is my gambling still feeling like entertainment? Am I within the budget I set for myself? Are the people I care about happy with the balance I'm striking? If the answer to any of these is no, treat it as a serious signal and adjust.

Finally, remember that everyone has bad weeks, bad months, even bad years. A single rough patch does not mean you have a permanent problem. What matters is the pattern over time and your willingness to take the warning signs seriously when they appear. The tools above exist to make taking action easier, not harder. Use them generously. We will be glad you did, and so will you.
